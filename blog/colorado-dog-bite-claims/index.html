Colorado Dog Bite Claims: Strict Liability, Statute of Limitations, and Your Rights

July 15, 2026 • By Attorney Mike Zara

Colorado consistently ranks among the states with the highest rates of dog bite injuries. According to data from the Insurance Information Institute, dog bites account for roughly one-third of all homeowner's insurance liability claims nationwide, and the average payout per claim has risen sharply in recent years. In Colorado, where outdoor culture, trail running, cycling, and dog-friendly communities bring people and animals into frequent contact, serious bite injuries happen every day across the Front Range and beyond. What many victims do not know is that Colorado law places dog owners in a uniquely difficult position: unlike in many other states, Colorado owners can be held strictly liable for bite injuries even if their dog had never shown any prior sign of aggression.

As a dog bite attorney based in Colorado, I represent victims of serious dog attacks throughout the state, from Denver and Aurora to Colorado Springs, Fort Collins, and the mountain communities along I-70. This guide explains how Colorado's dog bite statute works, what you need to prove, the critical deadlines you cannot afford to miss, and how insurance companies try to reduce or deny your claim.

Colorado's Strict Liability Dog Bite Statute

Colorado's dog bite law is codified at C.R.S. § 13-21-124. Under this statute, a dog owner is strictly liable for damages suffered by a person who is bitten by the owner's dog if two conditions are met: the person bitten was in a public place or was lawfully in a private place when the bite occurred, and the injury was proximately caused by the dog bite itself.

The phrase "strictly liable" carries enormous legal weight. In most personal injury cases, you must prove that the defendant was negligent, meaning they failed to act as a reasonably careful person would have. Strict liability eliminates that requirement entirely. You do not need to show that the owner knew the dog was dangerous. You do not need evidence that the dog had bitten or threatened anyone before. You do not need to prove the owner did anything wrong at all. If you were lawfully present and the dog bit you, the owner is responsible for your damages as a matter of law.

This is a significant departure from what is sometimes called the "one-bite rule" applied in some states, where an owner escapes liability for the first bite because they had no prior knowledge of the dog's vicious propensity. Colorado abolished that approach for bite injuries through this statute. Owners take on legal responsibility for their animal's conduct the moment they choose to keep a dog, regardless of the dog's history.

The statute covers bodily injury, which Colorado courts interpret broadly to include lacerations, puncture wounds, scarring, broken bones, nerve damage, infections, and psychological trauma resulting from the attack. The statute applies to all dog breeds. Colorado state law does not impose breed-specific restrictions, though some municipalities have historically enacted local breed ordinances that exist independently of the state statute.

Who Is "Lawfully" on the Property?

The statute protects people who are bitten in public places and those who are lawfully present in a private place when the bite occurs. This covers a wide range of situations. You are lawfully present if you were invited onto the property, whether expressly or implicitly, as a social guest, contractor, delivery worker, meter reader, or anyone else with a legitimate reason to be there. Mail carriers and package delivery drivers who are bitten while performing their duties on private property qualify. Neighbors who enter a yard after receiving an explicit or implicit invitation qualify.

Trespassers generally do not have the protection of the strict liability statute. If you were on the owner's property without permission and without a legal right to be there, you cannot invoke C.R.S. § 13-21-124. You would need to pursue a claim under a different theory, likely ordinary negligence, which requires proving the owner knew of the dog's dangerous tendencies. For most trespasser cases, this creates a substantially higher bar. However, there is a notable exception for young children: Colorado courts, consistent with the general approach in American tort law, typically apply heightened protections for children who enter property due to an attractive nuisance, even when they technically lack permission.

The Provocation Defense

The most commonly raised defense to a Colorado dog bite claim is provocation. Under C.R.S. § 13-21-124(3), an owner may assert as an affirmative defense that the person bitten provoked the dog. If the defense succeeds and the victim is found more than 50% at fault for provoking the attack, the victim recovers nothing under Colorado's modified comparative negligence rule, discussed below. If the victim was 50% or less at fault, damages are reduced proportionally.

Provocation is a fact-specific determination that frequently leads to contested litigation. Insurance adjusters and defense attorneys almost always argue some form of provocation when a claim is filed, regardless of the circumstances. Common arguments include claims that the victim approached the dog too quickly, reached toward the dog without caution, made eye contact, or made a sudden movement that startled the animal. Courts and juries evaluate these arguments against the objective standard of what constitutes provocation under Colorado law, not simply what made the dog react.

Children face a particularly important nuance here. Colorado courts generally recognize that young children, who lack the capacity to understand how their behavior might provoke an animal, should not be held to the same standard of care as adults. A toddler who pets a dog the wrong way is not engaging in legal provocation. This is a critical point in cases involving child victims, who account for a disproportionate share of serious dog bite injuries nationally and in Colorado.

Colorado's Two-Year Statute of Limitations

Under C.R.S. § 13-80-102(1)(a), personal injury claims in Colorado must be filed within two years of the date of the injury. For dog bite cases, the clock generally starts running on the day of the attack. If you do not file your lawsuit within two years, the court will dismiss your claim regardless of how serious your injuries are or how clear the liability may be. There are no exceptions for waiting to see how your injuries heal, for incomplete medical treatment, or for ongoing insurance negotiations.

Two years can feel like a long time during the immediate aftermath of a traumatic injury, especially when you are focused on surgeries, wound care, infection management, and therapy. But the practical reality is that the most valuable evidence in a dog bite case exists in the days and weeks immediately following the attack. Photographs of the wound before it heals, witness identification and statements, animal control investigation reports, the dog's vaccination and impoundment records, and the owner's insurance information all need to be gathered promptly. Waiting months to contact an attorney means losing access to evidence that can significantly affect your case outcome.

There is one important exception to the two-year deadline: claims involving minor victims. When a child is bitten and no guardian files on the child's behalf within the standard two-year window, the minor typically has until two years after turning 18 to file a claim. This exception does not prevent filing sooner, and filing promptly remains strongly advisable for all the evidentiary reasons described above. But it does provide a legal safeguard for child victims whose parents may not have been aware of their rights.

Colorado's Modified Comparative Negligence Rule

Colorado follows modified comparative negligence under C.R.S. § 13-21-111. Under this framework, your compensation is reduced by your own percentage of fault for the incident, and you are completely barred from recovery if you are found to be more than 50% at fault. In dog bite cases, comparative fault most commonly enters the picture through the provocation defense: if a jury finds you 30% responsible for provoking the attack, your damages are reduced by 30%. If they find you 51% responsible, you recover nothing.

This rule gives insurance companies a financial incentive to argue that bite victims contributed to their own injuries. You should expect that if you file a claim, the insurer will gather statements, investigate your conduct before the bite, and build an argument for some percentage of fault on your part. Having documentation of what actually happened, from witness accounts to medical records that describe the mechanism of injury, is essential to countering these arguments effectively.

The Role of Homeowner's and Renter's Insurance

The vast majority of dog bite claims in Colorado are paid through homeowner's or renter's insurance policies. Most standard homeowner's policies include personal liability coverage that extends to dog bite incidents, and the coverage limits are typically $100,000 to $300,000, though higher limits exist under umbrella policies. Renter's insurance often includes similar liability coverage for tenants who own dogs.

The practical implication for bite victims is that there is usually an insurance policy available to compensate you, even if the dog owner has limited personal assets. The challenge is that insurers actively manage these claims to minimize payouts. Adjusters may contact you quickly after the incident to take a recorded statement and offer an early settlement that does not account for the full extent of your injuries, particularly any long-term scarring, future surgical procedures, or psychological trauma.

Some policies exclude coverage for specific dog breeds identified as high-risk in the insurer's underwriting guidelines. If the owner's policy excludes the breed of dog that bit you, you may need to pursue the owner personally or identify alternative coverage. An attorney can help trace the available insurance and ensure you are not prematurely foreclosing claims against policies that apply to your situation.

Non-Bite Dog Attack Injuries in Colorado

Colorado's strict liability statute at C.R.S. § 13-21-124 applies specifically to dog bites. It does not cover all injuries caused by dogs. If you were knocked down by a large dog, injured when a dog lunged at you while you were on a bicycle or on foot, or harmed in another way that did not involve the dog's teeth breaking skin, the strict liability statute does not automatically apply. You would need to bring your claim under a negligence theory.

A negligence claim requires showing that the owner knew or should have known the dog had dangerous tendencies, that the owner failed to exercise reasonable care to control the dog, and that this failure caused your injury. Evidence of prior incidents, complaints to animal control, known aggressive behavior, violations of local leash laws, and the context of the encounter all become relevant to establishing negligence in these cases. Colorado municipalities and counties maintain leash laws that can establish negligence per se when a dog owner allows an animal off-leash in a restricted area and the animal causes injury. The Denver metro area, Colorado Springs, Boulder, and most Front Range communities have specific leash law ordinances worth investigating after any dog-related injury.

Animal Control Reports and Criminal Citations

After a dog bite, the responding animal control officer will typically conduct an investigation and may issue citations to the dog's owner, quarantine the dog for observation, or in severe cases seek a hearing to have the dog declared dangerous. The animal control report and any citations issued are valuable evidence in your civil claim. If the dog had prior incidents on record with animal control, those records can support arguments about the owner's knowledge of the animal's behavior and can be particularly important in non-bite negligence cases where the strict liability statute does not apply.

Request copies of all animal control reports related to the incident as soon as possible. Under Colorado's open records law, the Colorado Open Records Act (CORA) at C.R.S. § 24-72-201 et seq., you have the right to request records maintained by government agencies including animal services departments. Some agencies release these records directly; others require a formal CORA request. Your attorney can assist in obtaining these records efficiently.

Medical Documentation and Long-Term Damages

Dog bite injuries range from minor puncture wounds to catastrophic tissue damage requiring reconstructive surgery. Infection is a serious complication in all dog bites because the bacteria present in a dog's mouth, particularly Capnocytophaga canimorsus and Pasteurella species, can cause rapidly advancing cellulitis or systemic infection if not treated promptly with appropriate antibiotics. Victims should seek medical evaluation immediately, even for bites that appear superficial, and follow all prescribed treatment protocols carefully.

Facial injuries are particularly common when children are bitten, and the resulting scarring often requires multiple reconstructive procedures over years. Permanent scarring and disfigurement are compensable non-economic damages under Colorado law, and their value depends heavily on the location and severity of the scarring, the victim's age, and the impact on the victim's daily life and self-image. Psychological injuries, including post-traumatic stress disorder, phobia of dogs, anxiety, and depression, are also compensable and should be documented through appropriate mental health treatment.

Do not settle your claim before your medical treatment is complete or your condition is deemed stable by your treating physicians. Early settlements offered by insurance companies almost never account for the full cost of future care. Once you sign a release, you cannot reopen your claim regardless of how your injuries progress.

What to Do After a Colorado Dog Bite

Seek medical treatment immediately, even if the wound seems minor. Identify the dog's owner and obtain their contact information and the dog's vaccination records, particularly rabies vaccination status. Report the bite to local animal control or the county sheriff. Photograph your injuries before treatment and again throughout the healing process. Collect the names and contact information of anyone who witnessed the attack. Preserve any clothing or personal property damaged in the attack.

Do not provide a recorded statement to the dog owner's insurance company before speaking with an attorney. Insurers train adjusters to gather statements that can be used later to argue provocation or minimize the severity of your injuries. You have no legal obligation to give a recorded statement to the adverse insurer, and doing so before you understand your rights can significantly harm your case.

Contact a dog bite attorney promptly. Colorado's two-year statute of limitations may seem generous, but the practical window for preserving the best evidence and building the strongest case is much shorter. Early intervention by an attorney ensures that evidence is documented, insurance coverage is identified, and your medical treatment is properly positioned to support your claim.

What Compensation Is Available in Colorado Dog Bite Cases

If your dog bite claim succeeds, you can recover economic damages covering all past and future medical expenses, lost wages during your recovery, lost earning capacity if the injury is permanently disabling, and out-of-pocket costs related to the attack. Non-economic damages for pain and suffering, permanent scarring and disfigurement, emotional distress, and loss of enjoyment of life are also available. Colorado does cap non-economic damages in some civil cases, but the caps applicable to standard personal injury cases have specific thresholds and exceptions that your attorney can evaluate based on the facts of your situation. Punitive damages are available in cases involving willful and wanton conduct, though they are relatively rare in dog bite claims absent egregious circumstances such as a deliberate attack.
