Georgia Truck Accident Claims: FMCSA Violations, Shared Fault, and Your Rights

July 22, 2026 • By Attorney Mike Zara

Georgia sits at the crossroads of the American Southeast. Interstates 75, 85, and 20 converge in Atlanta, creating one of the most heavily trafficked freight corridors in the country. The Port of Savannah is the busiest container port on the East Coast, and its growth has placed tens of thousands of additional heavy commercial vehicles on Georgia roads every year. That volume comes with a predictable cost: Georgia regularly ranks among the top ten states nationally for fatal large truck crashes. When an 80,000-pound semi-truck collides with a passenger vehicle, the results are rarely minor.

As a truck accident attorney licensed in Georgia, I handle cases involving tractor-trailers, box trucks, tanker trucks, flatbeds, and other commercial vehicles throughout the state. This guide explains how Georgia law applies to these claims, what federal regulations govern the trucking industry, and what you need to do to protect your rights after a serious crash.

Why Truck Accident Cases Are Different from Car Accident Cases

A truck accident case is fundamentally different from a standard car accident claim. The most obvious difference is the severity of injury. The physics of a collision between a loaded semi-truck and a passenger vehicle mean that occupants of the smaller vehicle absorb an enormous amount of force. Traumatic brain injuries, spinal cord damage, internal organ injuries, multiple fractures, and crush injuries are common. Fatalities occur at far higher rates than in crashes between passenger vehicles.

Beyond the injury severity, truck accident cases involve a web of potentially liable parties that simply does not exist in typical car accident claims. The driver, the motor carrier, the shipper who loaded the cargo, the company responsible for vehicle maintenance, the manufacturer of defective components, and the broker who arranged the haul may all share responsibility for the crash. Identifying every responsible party and the insurance coverage available through each is a critical step that determines the maximum compensation available to an injured victim.

Truck accident cases also involve a distinct regulatory framework. The Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) imposes detailed requirements on commercial motor vehicle operators and carriers that go far beyond ordinary traffic law. When those regulations are violated and that violation contributes to a crash, the violation is strong evidence of negligence per se under Georgia law.

Key FMCSA Regulations and How Violations Build Your Case

The FMCSA's regulations are codified at 49 C.F.R. Parts 300–399 and cover virtually every aspect of commercial trucking. The regulations most frequently at issue in Georgia truck accident litigation include the following.

Hours of Service (HOS) Rules. Under 49 C.F.R. § 395.3, property-carrying drivers may not drive more than 11 hours following 10 consecutive hours off duty, and may not drive after 14 consecutive hours of being on duty. A mandatory 30-minute break is required after 8 hours of cumulative driving. Carriers must maintain driver logs documenting compliance, and since 2019, most commercial vehicles subject to HOS rules must use electronic logging devices (ELDs) rather than paper logs under 49 C.F.R. § 395.8. ELD data is highly accurate and difficult to falsify. If a driver was fatigued from exceeding allowable drive time, ELD records, GPS pings, fuel receipts, and toll records will document the violation.

Driver Qualification Standards. Under 49 C.F.R. Part 391, motor carriers must verify that drivers hold a valid commercial driver's license, have a satisfactory medical certificate from an FMCSA-registered examiner, pass drug and alcohol screening, and have an acceptable safety record. Carriers that place drivers with disqualifying violations, prior DUI convictions, or falsified medical certificates behind the wheel face significant negligent hiring and negligent entrustment exposure under Georgia law in addition to FMCSA penalties.

Vehicle Inspection and Maintenance. FMCSA regulations at 49 C.F.R. Part 396 require motor carriers to systematically inspect, repair, and maintain every commercial motor vehicle they operate. Pre-trip and post-trip driver inspection reports must be completed daily and retained. Brake failures, tire blowouts, and steering defects that cause crashes are frequently traceable to inspection records showing that a known defect was deferred rather than repaired. Those records are essential evidence.

Cargo Securement. Improperly loaded or unsecured cargo is a significant cause of serious truck crashes. FMCSA regulations at 49 C.F.R. Part 393 establish specific requirements for how cargo must be secured, distributed, and covered. When cargo shifts mid-transit or falls from a flatbed onto the roadway, the shipper and the carrier may both face liability depending on who loaded and inspected the load.

Drug and Alcohol Testing. FMCSA regulations at 49 C.F.R. Part 382 require pre-employment testing, random testing, post-accident testing, and return-to-duty testing for alcohol and controlled substances. After any crash involving a fatality or a driver who receives a citation, post-accident testing must be completed within eight hours for alcohol and 32 hours for controlled substances. Carriers that fail to conduct required post-accident testing or allow drivers back on the road before completing return-to-duty protocols face direct regulatory liability.

Georgia's Statute of Limitations for Truck Accident Claims

Under O.C.G.A. § 9-3-33, Georgia imposes a two-year statute of limitations on personal injury claims. That clock begins running on the date of the truck accident. For wrongful death claims brought by the family of a person killed in a truck crash, O.C.G.A. § 51-4-2 likewise imposes a two-year deadline running from the date of death.

Two years may feel like a long window while you are still in the hospital, but truck accident cases require extensive pre-suit investigation that takes time. Electronic logging device data, which is critical evidence in fatigue-related crashes, may be automatically overwritten by the carrier's systems within 30 to 90 days if a litigation hold letter is not sent immediately. Surveillance video from truck stops, weigh stations, and cameras along the route typically records over itself on rolling 30-day cycles. Accident reconstruction requires access to the physical vehicle before it is repaired. The carrier's attorneys are often on scene within hours of a serious crash, beginning the process of controlling the narrative and managing evidence.

Reaching out to a Georgia truck accident attorney within days of a crash, not months, is the difference between a case built on comprehensive evidence and a case built on whatever the carrier chose not to destroy.

Claims against a governmental entity for road design defects, maintenance failures, or signal malfunctions carry even shorter notice requirements. Under O.C.G.A. § 50-21-26, ante litem notice against a state entity must be given within 12 months of the date of loss. County and municipal entities have their own notice deadlines. Missing these notice requirements bars the claim entirely, regardless of how clear the liability is.

Georgia's Modified Comparative Negligence Rule

Georgia follows a modified comparative negligence system under O.C.G.A. § 51-12-33. If you are found to be 50% or more at fault for the accident, you cannot recover any damages. If you are less than 50% at fault, your total damages are reduced proportionally by your percentage of fault.

In truck accident cases, trucking companies and their insurers aggressively pursue comparative fault arguments as a primary defense strategy. Common claims include that the victim was speeding, merged unsafely, was following too closely, failed to account for a truck's blind spots, or made a sudden lane change. These arguments are especially prevalent in rear-end crashes and side-swipe collisions on multi-lane highways.

Countering these arguments requires thorough evidence: dashcam footage from the victim's vehicle, surveillance video from nearby businesses or highway cameras, witness accounts, cell phone records from the truck driver, and formal accident reconstruction. Georgia courts have held that a trucking company's failure to preserve evidence after reasonable notice of potential litigation can support a spoliation instruction to the jury, allowing jurors to draw adverse inferences from the missing evidence. That is a powerful tool in cases where the carrier has destroyed or failed to preserve electronic data.

Multiple Defendants and Insurance Layers

One of the most important practical advantages of truck accident cases compared to standard car accident claims is the availability of commercial insurance coverage in amounts that can actually compensate for serious injuries. Under FMCSA regulations, motor carriers operating in interstate commerce must carry minimum liability coverage of $750,000 for property-carrying vehicles and $5 million for certain hazardous materials carriers under 49 C.F.R. § 387.9. Many carriers carry policies well above these minimums.

Beyond the primary carrier's policy, the trucking industry's structure creates multiple layers of potential coverage. The freight broker who arranged the haul may carry its own liability coverage. The shipper or cargo owner may carry bailee or cargo liability coverage. The maintenance company that last serviced the vehicle's brakes may carry commercial general liability insurance. The truck's manufacturer may face a product liability claim if a component defect contributed to the crash. Building a complete picture of every available insurance policy is a task that requires careful investigation of corporate relationships, contractual agreements, and certificate of insurance records.

Georgia's direct action statute, codified at O.C.G.A. § 40-1-112, allows an injured person to sue the motor carrier's insurer directly in certain circumstances, which can simplify recovery when the carrier is insolvent, operating as a shell entity, or when multiple carriers dispute which of their policies was in effect at the time of the crash.

Georgia-Specific Trucking Hazards

The Atlanta metropolitan area presents unique truck accident risks that are worth understanding. The interchange of I-285 (the Perimeter) with I-75, I-85, and I-20 is among the most complex highway junctions in the country. Heavy trucks navigating these multi-lane, high-speed transitions in congested conditions create rollover and jackknife risks that are significantly higher than on open interstate stretches. The downtown connector where I-75 and I-85 merge is consistently ranked as one of the most congested roadways in the United States, and abrupt braking by commercial vehicles in that corridor is a recurring cause of rear-end chain collisions.

Outside Atlanta, Georgia's long-haul corridors along I-75 toward Macon and Valdosta and along I-16 connecting Macon to Savannah carry extremely high volumes of freight traffic serving the port. Fatigued driving is a documented risk on these routes, where long uninterrupted stretches of highway encourage drivers to push beyond legal hours-of-service limits.

Georgia weather compounds these risks. Ice events on elevated bridge decks during winter storms cause multi-vehicle pileups involving commercial trucks that cannot stop within normal distances on slick surfaces. Sudden summer thunderstorms produce standing water and flash hydroplaning conditions that are particularly dangerous for loaded trailers with worn tires.

Steps to Take After a Georgia Truck Accident

Call 911 immediately and wait for law enforcement to arrive. Georgia requires a police report for accidents involving injury, death, or significant property damage. The official report documents the responding officer's observations and any citations issued at the scene. Photograph all vehicles, their final resting positions, any tire marks, road debris, cargo on the roadway, and your own injuries before anything is moved.

Seek emergency medical evaluation even if you believe your injuries are minor. Adrenaline frequently masks pain, and internal injuries including traumatic brain injuries and abdominal organ damage may not produce obvious symptoms for hours or days. A contemporaneous emergency room record is foundational evidence of injury causation. Gaps in treatment are used by defense attorneys and insurers to argue that the injury was not serious or did not result from the accident.

Do not speak to the trucking company's insurer or sign any documents without first consulting an attorney. Trucking companies carry specialized claims teams trained to minimize payouts, and recorded statements taken in the days following a crash routinely produce admissions that are used to support comparative fault arguments at trial.

Contact a Georgia truck accident attorney as soon as possible so that a preservation demand can be sent to the carrier requiring retention of ELD data, driver logs, inspection records, dashcam footage, drug test results, maintenance records, and communications about the crash. Time is the most important variable in evidence preservation.

What Compensation Is Available in a Georgia Truck Accident Claim?

Georgia allows injured victims to recover both economic and non-economic damages. Economic damages include all past and future medical expenses, lost wages, diminished earning capacity, rehabilitation costs, in-home care expenses, and other out-of-pocket losses. Non-economic damages compensate for pain and suffering, mental anguish, permanent impairment, disfigurement, and loss of enjoyment of life. Georgia has no cap on compensatory damages in personal injury cases.

Punitive damages are available under O.C.G.A. § 51-12-5.1 when the defendant's conduct shows willful misconduct, malice, fraud, wantonness, or conscious indifference to consequences. Carriers that knowingly put fatigued or impaired drivers on the road, that falsify inspection records, or that continue operating vehicles with known safety defects face punitive damages exposure. Georgia caps punitive damages at $250,000 in most cases, but there is no cap when the defendant acted with specific intent to cause harm or was intoxicated.
